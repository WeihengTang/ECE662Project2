\begin{abstract}
This report presents a systematic study of the Orthogonal Low-Rank
Transformation (OLT), a linear embedding that jointly enforces
intra-class low rank and inter-class orthogonality through a single
nuclear-norm objective, optimised via the Concave--Convex Procedure
(CCCP).
\textbf{Part~1} validates OLT on toy data and Extended YaleB faces:
OLT with nearest-subspace classification outperforms LDA by
$\sim\!4$ points on 38-way face recognition, and a single
OLT-refinement step more than triples the Adjusted Rand Index
of spectral clustering ($0.055\!\to\!0.186$) despite being
initialised with $>\!77\%$ incorrect pseudo-labels.
\textbf{Part~2} extends OLT to modality-invariant feature learning
on Original and Rotated MNIST ($\pm 45^\circ$): a joint transform
that concatenates both modalities along the sample axis improves
cross-modality accuracy by $+16$ points over per-modality transforms.
When applied post-hoc to frozen CNN features, however, OLT
\emph{hurts} by $-1.8$ points relative to the raw nearest-subspace
baseline, revealing a mismatch between the linear low-rank prior
and nonlinear ReLU activations.
\textbf{Part~3} resolves this mismatch by integrating the OLT
functional as a differentiable regularizer during CNN training:
a modest penalty ($\lambda\!=\!0.1$) preserves clean-test accuracy
while improving rotated-test accuracy by $+0.7$ points and raising
feature-space orthogonality from $r\!=\!0.58$ to $0.81$.
Together, the three parts trace a clear arc from a well-founded
linear method through its limitations to a principled nonlinear
extension.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem formulation and OLT objective}

Given $C$ classes of samples $\Y = [\Y_1,\dots,\Y_C]$ where $\Y_c \in
\mathbb{R}^{d\times n_c}$ stacks the $n_c$ samples of class $c$ as
columns, the Orthogonal Low-rank Transformation (OLT) seeks a linear
map $\T \in \mathbb{R}^{d'\times d}$ that simultaneously
\emph{compresses each class to a low-rank subspace} and \emph{makes
those subspaces mutually orthogonal}. Both goals are encoded through a
single nuclear-norm based objective:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\T}\;\;
\sum_{c=1}^{C}\nuc{\T \Y_c}\;-\;\nuc{\T \Y}
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad \spn{\T}=1.
\label{eq:olt}
\end{equation}
The constraint $\spn{\T}=1$ rules out the trivial scaling $\T\to 0$.

\paragraph{Why this objective.} The nuclear norm $\nuc{A}=\sum_i
\sigma_i(A)$ is the tightest convex surrogate of $\mathrm{rank}(A)$, so
$\sum_c\nuc{\T\Y_c}$ acts as a class-wise rank surrogate
(\emph{intra-class compression}). Furthermore, for any block matrix
$[A,B]$ one has the standard inequality
\begin{equation}
\nuc{[A,B]}\;\le\;\nuc{A}+\nuc{B},\qquad
\text{with equality iff } \mathrm{col}(A)\perp\mathrm{col}(B),
\label{eq:perp}
\end{equation}
so the gap $\sum_c\nuc{\T\Y_c}-\nuc{\T\Y}\ge 0$, and the gap vanishes
\emph{exactly} when the transformed class subspaces are pairwise
orthogonal. Minimising the gap therefore drives both rank reduction and
inter-class orthogonality with one term.

\section{Optimization via the Concave--Convex Procedure (CCCP)}
\label{sec:cccp}

The objective in \eqref{eq:olt} is a difference of two convex functions
\[
f(\T)=u(\T)-v(\T),\qquad
u(\T)=\sum_c\nuc{\T\Y_c},\qquad
v(\T)=\nuc{\T\Y}.
\]
CCCP linearises the concave term $-v(\T)$ around the current iterate
$\T^{(t)}$. With the chain rule and the subgradient rule for nuclear
norms,
\[
\frac{\partial}{\partial \T}\nuc{\T\Y}\;=\;G\,\Y^{\top},
\qquad
\Y^{\top}\T^{\top}=U\Sigma V^{\top}\;\Rightarrow\;G=UV^{\top},
\]
the linearised subproblem becomes
\begin{equation}
\T^{(t+1)}=\argmin_{\T}\;\sum_c\nuc{\T\Y_c}\;-\;\mathrm{tr}\!\left(G\,\Y^{\top}\T^{\top}\right),
\quad\spn{\T}=1,
\label{eq:cccp_inner}
\end{equation}
solved by a few proximal subgradient steps followed by a projection
$\T\leftarrow\T/\sigma_{\max}(\T)$ to enforce the constraint. We
gradients are scaled by $1/N$ so that the step size is
dataset-size invariant.

\subsection{Task 2 -- YaleB classification, OLT vs.\ LDA}

\paragraph{OLT loss.} The loss used here is exactly the OLT objective
defined in~\eqref{eq:olt}, applied to the PCA-projected training
features: $\sum_c \nuc{\T\Y_c} - \nuc{\T\Y}$ subject to
$\spn{\T}=1$, optimised via CCCP as in Section~\ref{sec:cccp}. No
modification to the formulation is needed for classification; the
same low-rank plus orthogonality prior applies directly to the
face-illumination subspaces.

\paragraph{Setup.} Extended YaleB (38 subjects, 64 illumination
conditions each, $48\times 42$ pixels) is fetched. We use a stratified $50/50$
train/test split, project onto the top $150$ PCA components fit on the
training set ($98.8\%$ explained variance), and rescale features so the
mean row norm is $\approx 1$. The OLT solver targets $d_{\text{out}}=64$
with $60$ outer CCCP iterations and $8$ inner subgradient steps at
step size $0.5$.

\paragraph{Classification rule.} For every class $c$ we form an
orthonormal basis $B_c$ of the transformed training features (singular
energy~$\ge 0.95$), and assign each test sample $x$ to
$\hat{c}=\argmin_c \|(I-B_cB_c^{\top})\,\T x\|_2$ -- the
\emph{nearest-subspace classifier}. This rule is the natural inference
counterpart of the nuclear-norm-low-rank objective: it picks the class
whose linear subspace explains the test sample best.
For the LDA baseline, we use scikit-learn's Linear Discriminant Analysis,
which projects the data onto the $C\!-\!1$ most discriminative directions
and classifies via Bayes' rule under a shared-covariance Gaussian model
(equivalent to a linear decision boundary in the projected space).
All results are reported in Table~\ref{tab:yaleb_cls} and visualised in
Figure~\ref{fig:yaleb_acc}.

\paragraph{Comparison and analysis.}

\emph{When does OLT outperform LDA?}
OLT paired with the nearest-subspace classifier ($94.9\%$) outperforms
LDA ($91.0\%$) by $\sim\!4$ percentage points. The advantage arises
because of a fundamental modelling difference: LDA assumes each class
is a unimodal Gaussian cluster (one mean, shared covariance), while
OLT models each class as an entire \emph{subspace}. In YaleB, a
single subject's face under 64 different lighting conditions spans a
multi-dimensional illumination subspace rather than a compact point
cloud; the subspace assumption is a better structural match for this
data, and the nearest-subspace classifier exploits it at test time.

\emph{When does OLT fail?}
OLT paired with \emph{nearest-centroid} classification drops to
$74.9\%$, far below LDA. This makes sense: OLT deliberately spreads
each class across a low-rank subspace (that is its goal), so the class
mean becomes a poor representative. The nearest-centroid rule discards
exactly the within-subspace structure that OLT preserves. In contrast, sklearn's LDA classifier uses Bayes' rule under a shared-covariance
Gaussian model, producing a \emph{linear decision boundary} that is
well-matched to LDA's own projection. The lesson is that OLT
\emph{requires} a subspace-aware classifier (such as nearest-subspace)
to realise its benefit; a point-based rule (nearest centroid) negates
its geometric prior.

\emph{Further observations.}
Raw PCA features already work reasonably well with nearest-subspace
($84.3\%$), confirming that the subspace structure is present in the
original data. OLT's role is to \emph{purify} those subspaces by
suppressing within-class rank and rotating them toward orthogonality,
which narrows the gap between the nearest and second-nearest subspace
for each test sample and reduces misclassification. We also note that
running the CCCP solver too long (200 iterations vs.\ 60) causes the
training objective to shrink further but test accuracy to drop
($90.1\%$), suggesting the transformation over-collapses the
subspaces and loses generalisation -- an effect analogous to
overfitting that practitioners should be aware of.

\subsection{Task 3 -- Subspace clustering on YaleB}

We follow the project's prescription \emph{``convert clustering into
classification via OLT''}:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Build a symmetric $k=7$ nearest-neighbour graph on the PCA

pseudo-labels.
\item Train OLT (same hyper-parameters as Task 2) using the
pseudo-labels as supervisory targets.
\item Apply the nearest-subspace classifier in the OLT-transformed
space to reassign every sample. The reassigned labels are the OLT
clustering output.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
At first glance, an absolute accuracy of $0.350$ might appear modest.
However, it is crucial to contextualize this within the extreme
difficulty of the task: performing completely unsupervised clustering on
38 distinct subjects under severe illumination variations.

The baseline spectral clustering struggles significantly, yielding an
accuracy of only $0.229$ and an ARI of $0.055$. Since this accuracy is
measured after Hungarian matching (the best possible label permutation),
the raw pseudo-labels fed into the OLT training pipeline were at least
$77\%$ incorrect -- and likely worse.

The most remarkable observation is OLT's behavior under this massive
label noise. Instead of entirely overfitting to the erroneous
pseudo-labels, the nuclear-norm minimization acts as a powerful
structural prior. By forcing the data into orthogonal low-rank
subspaces, OLT is inherently robust to the label corruption introduced
by the flawed spectral initialization. Consequently, it
achieves a relative improvement of over $50\%$ in Accuracy and more than
triples the ARI ($0.055 \rightarrow 0.186$). This empirically
demonstrates that OLT is not merely learning to memorize its supervisory
signals, but is actively uncovering the intrinsic geometric structure of
the data.

\subsection{Task 1 -- Per-modality transforms}

We learn $\T_o$ on the original features and $\T_r$ on the rotated
features independently and evaluate each transform on both modalities
with the nearest-subspace classifier (results in
Table~\ref{tab:part2}, top two rows).

\subsection{Task 2 -- Joint transformation (feature concatenation)}
\label{sec:joint}

To enforce that both modalities live in the \emph{same} per-class
subspace, we concatenate the two modalities along the sample axis as
$\Y_c^{\text{joint}}=[\Y_c^{o},\Y_c^{r}]$ and apply the standard OLT
problem~\eqref{eq:olt}:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\T}\;\sum_{c=1}^{C}\nuc{\T \Y_c^{\text{joint}}}\;-\;\nuc{\T\Y^{\text{joint}}}.
\label{eq:joint}
\end{equation}
By \eqref{eq:perp} the minimum drives the joint per-class block to a
single low-rank subspace shared by both modalities, which is exactly
the modality-invariance property we want.
The cross-modality accuracy matrix is given in Table~\ref{tab:part2}
and visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:mnist_heatmap}.

\paragraph{Discussion: the vulnerability of single-modality transforms.}
The results in Table~\ref{tab:part2} clearly demonstrate the severe
limitations of learning modality-specific transformations in a
multi-modal setting.

First, we observe a drastic performance degradation during
cross-modality evaluation. The transform learned on original aligned
digits ($\T_o$) suffers a large drop (down to $0.4745$) when evaluated
on rotated digits. This confirms that the orthogonal low-rank subspaces
optimized for one modality are highly specific to that visual domain and
fail to generalize when the spatial geometry of the input is altered.

Second, and more fundamentally, learning a standalone transform on the
rotated modality ($\T_r$) yields poor performance even on its own
domain ($0.5155$). This occurs because the per-sample random rotation
destroys the inherent linear alignment (low-rank structure) of the
digits: instances of the same class are scattered across different
orientations, making it difficult for OLT to find a compact subspace.
Notably, $\T_r$ actually scores slightly higher on original digits
($0.5585$) than on rotated ones, suggesting that whatever partial
structure it does recover transfers better to the cleaner, aligned
domain -- further evidence that rotation disrupts per-class rank
structure.

These failures explicitly motivate the modality-invariant OLT
($\T_{\text{joint}}$) in Task~2, which forces the scattered
representations from different modalities to align into a shared
subspace, recovering the underlying low-rank structure across domains.

\paragraph{Discussion: why the joint transform succeeds.}
The joint transform $\T_{\text{joint}}$ dominates both per-modality
transforms on \emph{every} test condition (Table~\ref{tab:part2}):
$0.745$ vs.\ $0.698$ on original, and $0.636$ vs.\ $0.516$ on rotated.
Three factors explain this success.

First, the sample-axis concatenation
$\Y_c^{\text{joint}}=[\Y_c^{o},\Y_c^{r}]$ doubles the number of
samples available per class, giving the nuclear-norm objective a richer
view of within-class variation. The solver sees both the aligned and
the rotated instances of each digit simultaneously, so the subspace it
discovers must accommodate both --- this is precisely the
modality-invariance property encoded by~\eqref{eq:joint}.

Second, unlike the per-modality transforms that each overfit to one
domain's orientation statistics, the joint transform is forced to find
the \emph{common} low-rank structure shared across rotations. For
classes whose members naturally span a low-dimensional manifold
(e.g.\ stroke geometry of a digit ``3''), this shared structure exists
and is robust; the solver simply needs enough diversity in the training
pool to expose it.

Third, the $+16$-point lift on the cross-modality case
($\T_o\!\to\!\text{rot}$: $0.475\!\to\!0.636$) is especially
noteworthy because the joint transform was never explicitly optimised
for cross-domain transfer --- it was optimised for within-class
compression and between-class orthogonality on the pooled data. The
cross-modality gain is an emergent consequence of the shared-subspace
prior, confirming that OLT's geometric objective is well-suited to
modality-invariant representation learning even without an explicit
domain-alignment loss.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The results reveal a subtlety that the original two-row comparison
masked. Simply switching from the CNN's trained linear head (fc2) to the
nearest-subspace classifier on the raw 128-d features already boosts
rotated accuracy by $+3.5$ points ($86.39\%\to 89.89\%$), because the
subspace classifier is naturally more tolerant of within-class variation
than a point-wise linear boundary.

Against this fair baseline, the joint OLT transformation actually
\emph{hurts} by $-1.8$ points on the rotated set ($89.89\%\to 88.11\%$).
This holds across all tested output dimensions
($d_{\text{out}}\in\{32,64,96,128\}$), ruling out information loss as the
cause. The explanation
lies in a mismatch of assumptions: OLT's nuclear-norm prior assumes
that each class occupies a \emph{linear} subspace, which fits raw
pixels and PCA features well (Part~1, Part~2 Tasks~1--2) but not the
nonlinear, ReLU-activated CNN features. The CNN has already folded the
illumination/rotation manifold into a representation that is
well-clustered but not necessarily low-rank in the matrix sense;
imposing a linear low-rank constraint on top of this over-regularises
and discards useful nonlinear structure.

This negative result is itself informative: in this experiment, the OLT
linear prior is complementary to, rather than a replacement for, the
nonlinear feature learning done by CNNs. Part~3 explores a more
principled integration, where the OLT loss is applied \emph{during}
training so the network can co-adapt its features to satisfy both the
cross-entropy and the nuclear-norm objectives.

\section{Part 3 (Option A): OLT as a neural-network regularizer}

\subsection{Motivation}

Sections~3 and~4 establish OLT as a powerful linear post-hoc
embedding. A natural follow-up is to bake the same geometric prior
into the feature extractor itself: rather than retro-fit
low-rank/orthogonal structure with $\T$ after training, we ask the
network to learn features that are already low-rank intra-class and
orthogonal between classes.

\subsection{Loss function}

Let $f_\theta(x)\in\mathbb{R}^d$ be the penultimate feature of a CNN
classifier with linear head $W$. For a mini-batch $\mathcal{B}$ of
$|\mathcal{B}|$ samples with feature matrix $\F_{\mathcal{B}}\in
\mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{B}|\times d}$ and labels $y_{\mathcal{B}}$, define
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{OLT}}(\F_{\mathcal{B}},y_{\mathcal{B}})
\;=\;\frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\!\left(\sum_{c=1}^{C}\nuc{\F_{\mathcal{B},c}}-\nuc{\F_{\mathcal{B}}}\right),
\label{eq:olt_nn}
\end{equation}
the same nuclear-norm OLT functional as in \eqref{eq:olt}, applied
directly to the network's mini-batch features. The total training loss
is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}\;=\;\underbrace{\mathrm{CE}(W f_\theta(x),y)}_{\text{cross-entropy}}
\;+\;\lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{OLT}}(\F_{\mathcal{B}},y_{\mathcal{B}}),
\quad\lambda\ge 0.
\end{equation}
Because PyTorch's SVD is differentiable, the
gradient of $\mathcal{L}_{\text{OLT}}$ flows all the way back to
$\theta$.

\paragraph{Justification.}
\begin{itemize}
\item The first term in \eqref{eq:olt_nn} compresses each class to a
low-rank manifold, encouraging tighter, more discriminative
clusters.
\item Subtracting the global $\nuc{\F_{\mathcal{B}}}$ exploits
property~\eqref{eq:perp}: minimisation pushes the non-negative gap
toward zero, i.e.\ toward orthogonal class subspaces. Orthogonality
acts as a strong margin in the latent space, complementary to the
softmax decision boundary.
\item In contrast to additive margin losses (e.g.\ ArcFace), the OLT
regularizer is fully \emph{geometric}: it constrains the entire
class subspace, not only its mean direction. This makes it more
robust to within-class variation -- precisely the situation
modelled by rotation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental setup}

Same CNN architecture as Part~2 Task~3. Identical seeds, batch size,
optimizer (Adadelta, $\eta=1.0$, $\gamma=0.7$), and 10 training epochs
on Original MNIST. We sweep
$\lambda\in\{0.0,\,0.1,\,0.5,\,1.0\}$ and evaluate every variant on
the clean and rotated test sets, plus a feature-orthogonality
diagnostic
\[
r\;=\;\frac{\nuc{\F}}{\sum_c\nuc{\F_c}}\;\in\;[0,1],
\]
which equals $1$ exactly when the class feature subspaces are mutually
orthogonal. All results are collected in Table~\ref{tab:part3} and
Figure~\ref{fig:part3_sweep}.

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]\centering
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule

\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{The $\lambda$ trade-off exhibits a clear phase transition.}
Table~\ref{tab:part3} and Figure~\ref{fig:part3_sweep} reveal three
distinct regimes as $\lambda$ increases:

\emph{Regime~1 ($\lambda=0.1$): beneficial regularization.}
The OLT term is small enough that cross-entropy still dominates the
gradient signal, so the network learns semantically correct class
boundaries. At the same time, the nuclear-norm penalty nudges the
penultimate features toward orthogonal, low-rank class subspaces ---
raising $r$ from $0.58$ to $0.81$. The result is a modest but
consistent improvement: $+0.02$ pt on clean test data and $+0.71$ pt
on rotated test data. The rotated gain is larger because orthogonal
subspaces provide a wider margin between classes, which helps absorb
the within-class variance introduced by rotation.

\emph{Regime~2 ($\lambda=0.5$): geometric saturation.}
The orthogonality measure climbs to $r\!=\!0.947$, nearly perfect, but
accuracy stagnates at baseline levels ($0.9914$ clean, $0.8598$
rotated). The network can still learn correct labels, but the OLT loss
is now consuming most of the representational capacity to enforce
strict geometry. The features are well-structured but over-constrained:
the network has less freedom to place class subspaces where the
cross-entropy loss would prefer them, and the accuracy gain from
orthogonality is offset by the loss of representational flexibility.

\emph{Regime~3 ($\lambda=1.0$): collapse.}
The OLT term overwhelms cross-entropy entirely. The network converges
to features with near-perfect orthogonality ($r\!=\!0.971$) but
catastrophically poor accuracy ($79.3\%$ clean, $62.0\%$ rotated).
This confirms that the nuclear-norm objective, while geometrically
principled, carries \emph{no semantic supervision}: it shapes
\emph{how} features cluster but not \emph{which} features belong to
which class. Without a sufficiently strong cross-entropy signal, the
network finds a geometrically optimal but semantically arbitrary
arrangement.

\paragraph{Why $\lambda=0.1$ is the sweet spot.}
The key insight is that the OLT regularizer and cross-entropy are
\emph{complementary}, not redundant. Cross-entropy assigns each sample
to the correct class but says nothing about the geometric relationship
\emph{between} class representations --- two classes can have
arbitrarily overlapping feature distributions as long as a linear
boundary separates them. The OLT term fills this gap by encouraging
orthogonal, low-rank subspaces, which provides an implicit geometric
margin. At $\lambda=0.1$, this margin is just strong enough to help on
out-of-distribution data (rotated digits) without constraining the
network so tightly that it loses in-distribution accuracy. This
behaviour is analogous to $L_2$ weight decay, where a small penalty
improves generalisation but too much penalty underfits: OLT acts as a
\emph{structural} regularizer on the feature geometry rather than a
norm penalty on the weights.

\paragraph{Connection to Part~2 Task~3.}
The contrast between Part~2 Task~3 (OLT hurts on frozen CNN features)
and Part~3 ($\lambda\!=\!0.1$ helps) is instructive. Post-hoc OLT
imposes a linear low-rank constraint on features that were already
optimised for a different geometry by cross-entropy training; the
result is a destructive mismatch. End-to-end OLT regularization, by
contrast, allows the network to \emph{co-adapt} its features: the CNN
learns representations that are simultaneously useful for
classification (via CE) and geometrically well-structured (via OLT).
This resolves the negative result from Part~2 and confirms that the
nuclear-norm prior is valuable when it participates in training rather
than being retrofitted afterward.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The regularizer requires $C+1$ SVDs per
mini-batch, so per-step cost is roughly $1.5\!\times$ vanilla CE.
The method also assumes that each mini-batch contains enough samples
per class to estimate $\nuc{\F_c}$ stably --- relevant when $C$ grows
large. Finally, the optimal $\lambda$ is likely dataset-dependent;
more complex domains with higher within-class variability may require
a different balance between the two loss terms.

\section{Conclusion}

The three parts of this project trace a coherent arc through the
capabilities and limitations of the nuclear-norm OLT prior:

\paragraph{Part~1 validates OLT as a linear embedding.}
On toy data, OLT reduces inter-class direction overlap to near zero
and collapses each class to a rank-$1$ spectrum
(Figure~\ref{fig:toy3d}). On Extended YaleB, the same objective
outperforms LDA by $\sim\!4$ points in 38-way face classification
(Table~\ref{tab:yaleb_cls}) because the subspace model better matches
the illumination manifold than LDA's unimodal Gaussian assumption. In
unsupervised clustering, a single OLT refinement step more than
triples the ARI ($0.055\!\to\!0.186$; Table~\ref{tab:yaleb_clust})
despite being initialised with $>\!77\%$ incorrect pseudo-labels,
demonstrating that the nuclear-norm prior acts as a powerful structural
regularizer that is inherently robust to label noise.

\paragraph{Part~2 extends OLT to modality invariance --- and exposes
its limits.}
The joint sample-axis concatenation formulation~\eqref{eq:joint} lifts
cross-modality MNIST accuracy by $+16$ points over per-modality
transforms (Table~\ref{tab:part2}), confirming that OLT's geometric
objective is naturally suited to learning shared representations across
visual domains. However, when applied post-hoc to frozen CNN features,
OLT \emph{hurts} by $-1.8$ points relative to the fair
nearest-subspace baseline (Table~\ref{tab:frozen_cnn}), revealing a
fundamental mismatch: the linear low-rank prior fits raw pixels and
PCA features well but clashes with the nonlinear, ReLU-activated
geometry of CNN representations.

\paragraph{Part~3 resolves the mismatch.}
By integrating the OLT functional as a differentiable regularizer
during CNN training, the network co-adapts its features to satisfy
both the cross-entropy and nuclear-norm objectives simultaneously.
A modest $\lambda\!=\!0.1$ preserves clean-test accuracy, improves
rotated-test accuracy by $+0.7$ points, and raises feature-space
orthogonality from $r\!=\!0.58$ to $0.81$ (Table~\ref{tab:part3}).
The clear phase transition across $\lambda$ values
(Figure~\ref{fig:part3_sweep}) --- beneficial regularization at
$\lambda\!=\!0.1$, geometric saturation at $\lambda\!=\!0.5$,
collapse at $\lambda\!=\!1.0$ --- confirms that the OLT prior is
complementary to, not a replacement for, the discriminative
cross-entropy signal.

\paragraph{Broader takeaway.}
A single nuclear-norm functional --- $\sum_c\nuc{\T\Y_c}-\nuc{\T\Y}$
--- encodes both intra-class compression and inter-class orthogonality,
and the CCCP solver that optimises it is compact ($<\!200$ lines) and
effective across faces, digits, and CNN features. The project
demonstrates that this geometric prior is most powerful when it
\emph{participates} in learning (Part~1, Part~2 Tasks~1--2, Part~3)
rather than being imposed after the fact (Part~2 Task~3). This
distinction --- co-adaptation versus retro-fitting --- is the central
lesson of the report.
